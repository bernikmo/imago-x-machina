# Schotter Study

This study explores a reconstruction of *Schotter* by Georg Nees, one of the most influential works of early computer art.

## About Georg Nees

Georg Nees (1926–2016) was a German mathematician, engineer, and one of the pioneers of computer-generated art.

Working with plotters and programmed systems, Nees explored how algorithms could be used not only for technical purposes, but also for aesthetic expression. His work helped establish computer art as a new artistic field during the 1960s.

## About Schotter

*Schotter* ("Gravel") is one of Nees' best-known plotter drawings and was created in the mid-1960s. The work has become a classic example of generative art and is frequently recreated as a creative coding exercise because of its conceptual clarity and visual strength.

The image consists of a regular grid of transparent squares. At first glance the system appears simple, yet it demonstrates a powerful visual transition from order to disorder.

## Historical Perspective

Today, recreating a system like *Schotter* is relatively accessible with tools such as Processing. A few lines of code are enough to generate, modify, and instantly preview countless variations.

When Georg Nees created the original work in the 1960s, the situation was fundamentally different. Computer access was limited, graphical output devices were rare, and visual experimentation required considerably more effort and planning than it does today.

This study is therefore not only an exploration of a famous artwork, but also a reminder of how pioneering these early experiments were. The ability to investigate such ideas so easily today is made possible by decades of technological development and by tools such as Processing, which have brought creative coding within reach of a much wider audience.

## Analysis

The composition is based on the interaction between:

* order and disorder
* repetition and variation
* structure and randomness

The system consists of:

* 22 rows
* 12 squares per row
* identical square dimensions
* transparent forms with visible outlines

The first row is perfectly ordered.

With each subsequent row the amount of disturbance increases. The squares gradually shift away from their original positions and orientations, creating an increasingly unstable structure.

One particularly interesting characteristic is that the vertical displacement appears stronger than the horizontal displacement, contributing to the sensation of collapsing order.

## Study Goals

This reconstruction focuses on understanding:

* how small algorithmic changes affect perception
* how randomness can be controlled through rules
* how visual order gradually transforms into chaos
* why a relatively simple system produces such a strong aesthetic effect

Rather than creating an exact copy, the goal is to explore the underlying principles of the original work and develop new variations from them.
